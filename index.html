What's your nest egg target?

Social Security doesn't start the day you retire — it starts whenever you choose to claim it. If those two dates don't match, you need extra savings to cover the gap between them.

The 4% rule: save roughly 25× what you plan to spend each year, and you can pull out about 4% annually without running out — a common starting point for a ~30-year retirement. Not a guarantee, just a solid baseline.
Say you retire at 55 but don't claim Social Security until 67. That's 12 years where Social Security pays you nothing — your own savings have to cover 100% of your spending during that stretch. We call that gap the "bridge." Once Social Security actually starts, it covers part of your spending, so the rest of your savings don't have to work as hard, or as long.

How much to put away monthly?

Working backward from your number, your timeline, and an assumed investment return.

Use an inflation-adjusted (real) return, not a raw market average — your spending targets are in today's dollars, so growth needs to be too. ~6-7% real is roughly the long-run historical figure for an all-stock portfolio; many planners now use more conservative forward-looking numbers like 4-5% real, especially for a balanced portfolio or a shorter timeline.
Include long-term investments you expect to use for retirement or financial freedom — such as a 401(k), IRA, brokerage account, or invested HSA.
